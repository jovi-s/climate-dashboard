## An AI Summary of Singapore’s Second Nationally Determined Contribution (NDC) 2025

1. **Emissions Reduction Targets**
   - **2035 Target:** Limit greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions to 45–50 MtCO₂e.
   - **Long-Term Goal:** Achieve net-zero emissions by 2050 (subject to technology maturity and international collaboration).

2. **Timeframe & Scope**
   - **Timeframe:** Single-year target set for 2035, with implementation from 2021–2035.
   - **Economy-Wide Coverage:**
     - **Sectors:** Energy, Industry, Agriculture, Land Use, Land-Use Change and Forestry (LULUCF), and Waste.
     - **Gases:** CO₂, CH₄, N₂O, HFCs, PFCs, SF₆, and NF₃.

3. **Key Strategies & Measures**
   - **Energy Transition**
     - **Natural Gas & Renewables:** Continue switching to natural gas and maximize solar deployment (target of ~2 GWp by 2030).
     - **Electricity Imports:** Aim to import up to 6 GW of low-carbon electricity by 2035 (~33% of demand).
     - **Phasing Out Coal:** Coal currently comprises <1% of Singapore’s energy mix and will be phased out by 2040.
     - **Emerging Technologies:** Explore hydrogen, carbon capture and storage (CCS), geothermal (where feasible), and other clean energy solutions.
   - **Carbon Pricing**
     - **Progressive Carbon Tax:** Covers ~80% of emissions.
     - **Planned increases through 2030; specific rates vary between the two summaries (e.g., S$50–80/tCO₂e by 2030).**
   - **Transport & Buildings**
     - **Transport:** Phase out internal combustion engine vehicles by 2040, expand public transport (rail network targeted to reach 360 km by 2030).
     - **Buildings:** Mandate energy efficiency audits and standards to reduce consumption and lower emissions.
   - **Domestic Institutional Arrangements**
     - **Inter-Ministerial Committee on Climate Change (IMCCC):** Oversees policy, chaired by a senior minister.
     - **National Climate Change Secretariat (NCCS):** Serves as the main coordinating body for climate strategies.

4. **Methodological Approaches**
   - **Emissions Accounting:** Follows 2006 IPCC Guidelines and higher-tier methods where relevant.
   - **Reporting:** Emissions inventory and Biennial Transparency Reports will be prepared according to UNFCCC guidance, with 2035 data reported by 2038.

5. **Challenges & Fairness**
   - **National Constraints:** Small land area, high population density, and limited renewable energy potential (no viable wind/hydro).
   - **Energy Dependency:** ~95% reliance on imported natural gas and vulnerability to global supply chains.
   - **High Costs:** Large upfront investments in clean energy infrastructure, including a multibillion-dollar “Future Energy Fund.”
   - **Fairness & Ambition:** Despite constraints, Singapore has been early in adopting measures like carbon taxes and is ramping up efforts consistent with global climate objectives.

6. **International Cooperation**
   - **Cross-Border Electricity:** Projects and pilot trades with ASEAN nations and beyond (e.g., Australia, Cambodia).
   - **Climate Finance & Capacity Building**
     - **Financing Asia’s Transition Partnership (FAST-P):** Mobilizes up to US$5 billion for Asia’s green transition.
     - **Singapore Cooperation Programme (SCP):** Trains officials from developing countries in sustainability and climate change.
   - **Global Initiatives:** Singapore joins international pledges on hydrogen and energy storage, and contributes to methane reduction efforts.

7. **Adaptation & Resilience**
   - **Adaptation Efforts:** Coastal protection, urban cooling, and water resource management are integrated into long-term planning.
   - **Adaptation Communication:** Details provided in Singapore’s First Adaptation Communication, covering climate resilience, resource efficiency, and economic diversification.

**Conclusion**

Singapore’s Second NDC strengthens its commitment to climate action through clear emissions targets (45–50 MtCO₂e by 2035) and a longer-term net-zero goal by 2050. Despite geographic and economic constraints, the country adopts a multi-faceted strategy—including a progressive carbon tax, energy diversification, cross-border collaboration, and robust adaptation measures—to fulfill its obligations under the Paris Agreement and support broader global decarbonization efforts.
